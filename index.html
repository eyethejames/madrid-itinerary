Introduction

Madrid today is a city dense with monuments, institutions, and public spaces whose origins reach back to the Early Modern period, when Spain emerged as one of the world’s first global empires. Although Madrid lacked the ancient pedigree of cities like Rome or Paris, it was deliberately transformed into an imperial capital from the sixteenth century onward. As the permanent seat of the Spanish monarchy under the Habsburgs and later the Bourbons, Madrid became a political, religious, financial, and intellectual center where imperial authority was asserted, ritualized, and remembered. The city’s urban fabric thus offers a unique lens through which to examine how empire was built and how its memory was preserved in everyday space.

This project approaches Madrid as a historical landscape shaped by empire, using an interactive map to guide viewers through nine sites that reveal different dimensions of imperial power. Rather than presenting empire as a distant or abstract phenomenon, the project emphasizes how imperial history was embedded in churches, palaces, plazas, scientific institutions, and centers of governance. These spaces materialized ideas of royal legitimacy, religious mission, global domination, and knowledge production, making empire visible and tangible within the city itself.

To structure this exploration, the project is divided into three thematic pathways. The first theme focuses on the foundations of imperial authority and early imperial memory, especially the symbolic importance of 1492, monarchy, and religious justification. The second theme examines how imperial power was sustained and commemorated through elite culture, urban planning, and financial institutions, revealing how empire shapedMadrid’s civic and social life. The third theme traces networks of knowledge, science, and global exchange, highlighting how information, objects, and resources from across the empire flowed into the capital and were systematized there.

Together, these themes create a connected narrative that allows Madrid to be read both as a physical city and as an imperial archive. By moving through these sites, the viewer is invited to reconsider familiar landmarks as products of empire and to reflect on how early modern imperial power continues to shape the city’s identity today

Conclusion

Viewed together, the sites presented in this project demonstrate that Madrid was not merely the administrative center of the Spanish Empire, but one of its most important symbolic and cultural engines. Through monuments, institutions, and urban spaces, imperial authority was continuously produced, reinforced, and remembered. The three thematic pathways show how empire permeated multiple aspects of city life: from royal imagery and religious justification, to elite display and financial power, to scientific inquiry and historical knowledge. Empire was not confined to distant colonies; it was lived, curated, and normalized in the metropolitan center.

The first theme reveals how the origins of empire were framed through celebratory narratives of monarchy, faith, and discovery. Monuments and religious spaces encouraged viewers to understand imperial expansion as a moment of unity and moral purpose, masking the violence and dispossession that accompanied conquest. The second theme illustrates how imperial power became embedded in Madrid’s urban form and elite culture, as aristocratic families, financial institutions, and monumental architecture transformed the city into a stage for authority, hierarchy, and continuity. The third theme shows how knowledge itself became an instrument of empire, as colonial nature, objects, and histories were collected, classified, and interpreted within metropolitan institutions that privileged European perspectives.

Taken together, these sites highlight the selective nature of imperial memory. While Madrid’s landscape prominently commemorates exploration, governance, and intellectual achievement, it largely excludes indigenous voices and colonial experiences. This absence is itself revealing, reminding us that public memory is shaped by power and purpose. Empire persists not only in what is displayed, but also in what is left unspoken.

By encouraging viewers to move through Madrid with critical attention to its imperial past, this project invites reflection on how history remains embedded in urban space. Reading the city through its imperial layers allows us to better understand how early modern empire shaped Madrid’s development and how its legacies continue to influence national identity, memory, and public space in the present.
